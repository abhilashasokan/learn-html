Types of Networks

Networks, which consist of various combinations of computers, storage devices, and communications devices, may be divided, based on their geographic range.

Personal Area Network (PAN)

PAN is the smallest type of network. This type of computer network, connects a computer to a number of devices like printers, scanners etc.

A personal area network (PAN) is the interconnection of devices within the range of an individual person, typically within a range of 10 meters. Typically, these systems are wireless and involve the transmission of data between devices such as smartphones, personal computers, tablet computers, etc.

Local Area Network (LAN)

A Local Area Network (LAN) connects computers and devices in a limited geographic area, such as one office, one building, or a group of buildings close together.

A LAN can be small or large, ranging from a home network with one user to an enterprise network with thousands of users and devices in an office or school. Regardless of size, a LAN's single defining characteristic is that it connects devices that are in a single, limited area

Computer networks have at least one host computer (server) and nodes (clients). A host computer is a main frame or midsize central computer that controls the network. The other devices within the network are called nodes. A node is any device that is attached to a network, a storage device or a printer.

Types of LANs

There are two principal types of local area networks.

  • Client/Server LANs : Client/Server LAN consists of clients, which are microcomputers that requests data, and server, which are computers to supply required information. The server is a powerful microcomputer that manages shared devices. Different types of servers may be used to manage different tasks.
  • Peer-to-peer LANs : A peer-to-peer LAN includes all microcomputers on the network that communicates directly with one another without relying on a server. Every computer can share files and peripherals with all other computers on the network, given that all are granted access privileges. Peer-to-peer networks are less expensive than client/server networks and effectively in small networks.

Campus Area Network (CAN)

A Campus Area Network (CAN) is a network of multiple interconnected Local Area Networks (LAN) in a limited geographical area. This type of computer network connects computers in a number of buildings. A campus Area Network (CAN) is ideal for a university but they are also found in industrial facilities (where it is known as Corporate Area Network). A CAN is smaller than a wide area network (WAN), but larger than Local area network (LAN).

Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

A metropolitan area network (MAN) is a communication network covering a city or a suburb. Its geographic scope falls between a WAN and LAN. MANs provide Internet connectivity for LANs in a metropolitan region, and connect them to wider area networks like the Internet.

Wide Area Network (WAN)

A wide area network (WAN) is a communications network that covers a wide geographic area, such as a country or the world. A WAN may use a combination of satellites, fibre-optic cable etc. and link a variety of computers, from mainframes to terminals. WANs are used to connect LANs/MANs together. The best example of WAN is the internet. Networks may be connected together- LANs to MANs and MANs to WANs. 'Backbones' are high-speed networks that connect LANs and MANs to the internet.

Benefits of networks

The most important reason for using a network is for communication and sharing of resources.

  1. Sharing of Peripheral devices: Using peripheral devices such as laser printers, disk drives, and scanners for each computers can be expensive. Networking helps to connect the peripherals to a network, serving several computer users, maximising their use.
  2. Sharing of Programs and Data: Networking helps to reduce the cost of software when people use the same application in a shared network. Also, if all employees have access to a database shared on a network, the organisation can save time and money.
  3. Better Communications: Networking helps better means of communication between people and computers at work. Client/server communication channels, e-mails, etc., helps in better communication. Network linked employees can work together more easily online on shared projects.
  4. Security of Information: Since the databases and other valid information repositories are spread across different computers, high network data security can be ensured. Loss of data due to any natural calamity or disintegrity of any particular employee can be avoided.
  5. Access to External databases: Network enable user to access numerous databases, whether private company databases or public databases available online through internet or extranet or virtual private networks.
